---
title: "Learning to Swim"
level: "a2"
date: "2026-04-22"
---

I always wanted to learn how to swim. When I was a child, I was afraid of water. Now I am twenty-five, and I decided it was time to face my fear.

I found a swimming school near my house. The teacher's name is Mike. He has been a swimmer for many years, and he teaches adults who are afraid of water.

On my first day, I went to the pool early. I sat by the water and watched other people swim. They looked so free and happy. I wanted to feel that way too.

Mike came over and shook my hand. "Welcome! Do not worry," he said. "Many adults come here afraid. By the end of our classes, you will swim like a fish."

I was not sure I believed him, but his smile made me feel better.

"Today we will start slowly," Mike said. "First, sit on the edge of the pool and put your feet in the water."

I did what he said. The water was cold at first, but after a minute it felt fine.

"Good! Now splash the water with your feet. Get used to the feeling."

I moved my feet up and down. The water made a funny sound. It was not scary at all.

After that, Mike taught me to blow bubbles. I put my face in the water and blew air out of my mouth. It felt strange, but I did it.

"Excellent!" Mike said. "You are doing great."

Next, he showed me how to float. I held the side of the pool and stretched my body. Mike held my stomach to help me.

"Relax," he said. "Let the water hold you. Do not fight it."

I tried to relax, but my body was stiff. I did not trust the water yet.

"Try again," Mike said. "Take a deep breath and let go."

I took a breath and tried one more time. This time, I felt my legs come up. I was floating! It was a strange but wonderful feeling.

"I did it!" I said, smiling.

"Yes, you did!" Mike said. "See? You can swim already."

For the next few weeks, I came to the pool every Tuesday and Thursday. Mike taught me many things: how to kick my legs, how to move my arms, and how to breathe while swimming.

Some days were harder than others. Sometimes water went up my nose, and I coughed. Sometimes I felt tired and wanted to stop. But Mike always encouraged me.

"You are stronger than you think," he said. "Keep trying."

One day, Mike told me to swim from one side of the pool to the other. I was scared.

"I cannot do it," I said. "It is too far."

"Yes, you can," Mike said. "I will swim next to you. You will be safe."

I took a deep breath and started. I kicked my legs and moved my arms. I remembered to breathe. I kept my eyes on the other side.

After twenty seconds, my hand touched the wall. I did it! I swam across the pool!

"Congratulations!" Mike said. "You are officially a swimmer now."

I was so happy I almost cried. All my fear was gone. In its place, I felt pride and joy.

Now I go swimming every week. Sometimes I swim alone, and sometimes I join a class. I even taught my sister how to float last month.

Learning to swim taught me something important: it is never too late to learn something new. Fear can be strong, but with practice and help, anyone can overcome it.
