# The Last Bookshop in Lisbon

When I first visited Lisbon, I was not looking for anything special. I was a tourist with a camera and a map, ready to see the famous yellow trams and the old castle on the hill. But on my third day in the city, I found something that changed my trip **completely**. It was a small bookshop on a quiet street, far from the busy tourist areas.

The shop had no name on the door, only a small sign that said "Books" in faded letters. The windows were dirty, and the paint on the walls was peeling. Most people would walk past without looking twice. But something made me stop. Perhaps it was the smell of old paper that came through the door, or perhaps it was the strange silence of the street on that hot afternoon.

I pushed open the door, and a little bell rang. Inside, the shop was dark and cool. Books were everywhere—on shelves, on tables, even in piles on the floor. There was no order that I could see. Novels sat next to science books. Children's stories leaned against thick history volumes. It looked like chaos, but it felt like a treasure house.

An old man sat behind a wooden desk near the back. He had white hair and wore glasses that were too big for his face. He looked up when I entered, but he did not smile. He only nodded and returned to his book. I liked that. In most tourist shops, people try to sell you things immediately. This man seemed to say: "Look if you want. Leave if you want. The books will still be here."

I started to explore. The books were in many languages—Portuguese, English, French, Spanish, and some I did not **recognize**. Many of them were very old. I opened a blue cover and saw that it was printed in 1923. The pages were yellow and soft, like old fabric. I was afraid to touch them too hard.

After twenty minutes, the old man spoke. "You are not looking for a specific book," he said. It was not a question.

"No," I answered. "I am just looking."

"Good," he said. "The best books find you when you are not searching."

I smiled and continued to look. I found a small book of poems by a Portuguese writer I had never heard of. The pages had notes written in the margins by someone who had owned the book long ago. I could not read all the handwriting, but I could see that the previous owner had loved these poems. There were little stars next to some lines, and question marks next to others.

"How much is this?" I asked, holding up the book.

The old man looked at it for a long moment. "Three euros," he said.

"Only three?" I was surprised. The book was from 1965 and was in perfect condition.

"Books have no fixed price," he said. "They are worth what someone is willing to pay for the story inside. You want that book. That means it has value for you. Three euros is enough."

I paid him and put the book carefully in my bag. "Do many people come here?" I asked.

"Not many," he said. "Young people buy electronic books now. They do not want paper anymore. They do not want to smell the pages or feel the weight of a story in their hands."

"That is sad," I said.

"Yes," he agreed. "But I am old now. When I die, this shop will probably become a café or a clothing store. That is progress, people say."

I did not know what to say. The idea of this beautiful place disappearing made me feel heavy.

"How long have you had this shop?" I asked.

"Forty-seven years," he said. "I came here when I was twenty-five. My father owned it before me. His father owned it before him. Three generations of my family have sold books on this street."

"That is amazing," I said.

"It is just life," he replied. "Families do things. Then they stop. New families start new things."

I stayed for another hour, talking with the old man. He told me about Lisbon in the old days, before so many tourists came. He told me about the writers who used to visit his shop—some famous, some forgotten. He made me coffee in a small kitchen at the back of the shop, and we drank it surrounded by books.

When I finally left, the sun was setting. The street looked different in the golden light. I turned back to look at the shop one more time. The old man was sitting at his desk again, reading, with a small lamp burning beside him. He did not look up.

I kept that book of poems. When I returned home, I tried to translate some of them. I learned that the poet had died young, only thirty-two years old. He wrote about the sea, about love, and about the sadness of beautiful things that do not last. Reading his words in that old book, with the notes of the previous owner beside me, I felt connected to something larger than myself.

Two years later, I returned to Lisbon. I walked down that same quiet street, looking for the bookshop. But it was gone. In its place, there was a bright new café with glass windows and modern furniture. Young people sat inside, looking at their phones and drinking expensive coffee.

I stood there for a long time, feeling empty. Then I remembered what the old man had said: "Families do things. Then they stop. New families start new things."

Progress, people say. But I still have his book. And sometimes, late at night, I open it and smell the old pages, and I am back in that dark shop, talking with a man who understood that stories are more important than money.

That is why I visit second-hand bookshops whenever I travel. Not because I need more books—I have too many already. But because each shop is a small **kingdom** of memories, and the people who keep them are guardians of something precious. We need to thank them while they are still there. We need to **recognize** that when they close their doors for the last time, a little light goes out of the world.
