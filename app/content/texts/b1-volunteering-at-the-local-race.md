---
title: "Volunteering at the Local Race"
level: "b1"
date: "2026-04-30"
---

Every spring, my town organizes a five-kilometer race to collect money for the public hospital. I had seen the race many times from the sidewalk, but I had never helped. This year, my friend Maya asked me to volunteer with her. At first, I said no because I am not a runner and I did not know what I could do. Maya laughed and told me that races need more than fast legs.

On the morning of the race, we arrived at the main square at seven o'clock. The air was cold, and many volunteers were already working. Some people were putting up signs. Others were preparing tables with water, fruit, and numbers for the runners. A woman named Carla gave us orange vests and explained our job.

"You will stand near the second corner," she said. "Show runners where to turn, and make sure cars do not enter the street. If someone needs help, call me."

It sounded simple, but I felt nervous. What if I sent people the wrong way? What if a driver became angry? Maya noticed my face and said, "Do not worry. We only need to pay attention."

At eight thirty, the first runners started to arrive near our corner. Some looked very serious, with expensive shoes and watches. Others came with families, children, and even dogs. One older man wore a shirt that said, "I run slowly, but I finish." That made many people smile.

When the race began, everything happened quickly. The fastest runners reached us in only a few minutes. I pointed to the left and shouted, "This way! Good job!" Maya clapped and repeated, "Keep going!" After the first group, more and more people came. Some were tired already, but they smiled when they heard us.

A small problem happened after twenty minutes. A delivery van tried to turn into the closed street. The driver looked annoyed because he had packages to leave nearby. I walked to his window and explained that the race would pass for only a short time. He sighed, but then he parked and waited. When a group of children ran past, he opened his window and cheered for them. Sometimes people change their mood when they feel part of something.

Later, a young runner stopped near us and held his side. He said he felt dizzy. Maya gave him water while I called Carla. The medical team came quickly. The runner was fine, but he decided not to continue. He looked disappointed, so Maya told him, "Stopping today is also a smart choice. You can run again next year." He nodded, and I think her words helped.

After the last walkers passed, we returned to the square. The place was full of music, food, and tired but happy people. Carla thanked all the volunteers. She said the event had collected more money than last year. I felt proud, even though my job had been small.

Before that day, I thought volunteering meant doing something big or difficult. Now I understand that many useful actions are simple. Standing at a corner, giving clear directions, offering water, and staying calm can help an event work well. If everyone gives a little time, the result can be much bigger than one person's effort.

Next spring, I will volunteer again. I may even try to run the race. I will probably be slow, but I already know that finishing is not the only important thing. Being there for other people matters too.
