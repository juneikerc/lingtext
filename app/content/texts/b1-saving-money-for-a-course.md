---
title: "Saving Money for a Course"
level: "b1"
date: "2026-05-18"
---

At the beginning of this year, I decided that I wanted to take a photography course. I have always enjoyed taking pictures with my phone, especially of streets, food, and people in normal places. However, I knew very little about light, color, or how to tell a story with a photo. The course looked interesting, but it cost more money than I had. Instead of forgetting the idea, I made a plan.

First, I wrote down how much money I received each month and how much I usually spent. I was surprised by the result. I did not buy expensive things, but I bought many small things without thinking: coffee after school, snacks at the station, and phone games when I was bored. Each thing was cheap, but together they became a lot of money.

I decided to save a small amount every week. If I saved too much, I knew I would stop after a few days. So I chose a number that was possible. I also put a picture of a camera on my wall. When I wanted to buy something I did not need, I looked at the picture and asked myself, "Do I want this snack more than the course?" Sometimes the answer was yes, but often it was no.

My friends helped me too. One friend invited me to walk in the park instead of going to a cafe. Another friend lent me an old book about photography. We started taking pictures together on Saturday mornings. It was free, and it was more fun than shopping. Although I was saving money, I did not feel lonely or bored.

After three months, I had enough money for the course. On the first day, I felt nervous because some students had real cameras and I only had my phone. But the teacher said that the most important thing was not the camera. It was learning to see. She showed us how a simple window, a chair, or a cup of tea could become an interesting photo if we looked carefully.

Now I am glad I saved the money. The course taught me photography, but it also taught me patience. I learned that a goal becomes easier when it is clear and when you take small steps. I still buy coffee sometimes, and I still enjoy snacks with friends. The difference is that I now think before I spend. That habit may help me with many future plans.
