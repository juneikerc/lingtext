# The Small Light in My Room

My name is Nina. I am eleven years old. I live in a small town with my mother, my father, and my brother Tom. Our house is near a school and a park. It is not a big house, but it is warm and happy. We have a kitchen, a living room, two small bedrooms, and a little garden with grass and flowers. My room is small too. I have a bed, a desk, a chair, a lamp, and a shelf with books. I like my room very much because it is quiet, simple, and full of my favorite things.

Every day I wake up at seven o’clock. I open my eyes, sit up in bed, and look at the window. In the morning, the light is soft and yellow. I hear birds outside. Sometimes I hear my mother in the kitchen. Sometimes I hear my brother in the hall. He is loud in the morning. He opens doors fast, walks fast, and talks fast. I am not like that. In the morning, I am slow and quiet.

I get out of bed and make my bed. Then I wash my face and brush my teeth. I put on my school clothes. I wear a skirt or jeans, a T-shirt, and a sweater when it is cold. Then I go to the kitchen. My mother is there. She smiles and says, “Good morning, Nina.” I say, “Good morning, Mum.” My father is often at the table with a cup of coffee. Tom eats bread very fast and talks about school, football, games, and many other things.

We eat breakfast together. I like bread with butter and jam. Sometimes we have eggs. Sometimes we have fruit. I like apples and bananas. My father likes oranges. My mother likes tea. I like milk in the morning. At breakfast, we talk about our day. My father goes to work. My mother works at home and in a shop some days. Tom and I go to school. Our school is not far, so we walk.

One day, on a Monday night, something strange happens.

That evening is very normal at first. I do my homework at my desk. I read a short story, write some answers, and draw a small picture for art class. Then I eat dinner with my family. We have rice, chicken, and salad. After dinner, Tom watches television. My father reads. My mother cleans the kitchen. I go to my room because I want to read my book before bed.

My room is quiet. The window is closed. The curtain is still. My lamp is on, and the light is warm. I sit on my bed and open my book. It is a book about animals. I read about birds, fish, and little brown mice. I like the pictures very much. After some time, I close the book and put it on the shelf.

Then I see it.

There is a small light under my desk.

I stop and look at it. My lamp is on my desk, but this light is not from the lamp. It is under the desk, near the floor, close to the wall. It is very small. It is white and soft. It does not move fast. It does not go out. It is just there.

I stand up slowly.

I look at the door. It is closed. I look at the window. It is closed too. I look at the small light again. My heart goes fast. I am not very afraid, but I am surprised. I go down on my knees and look under the desk.

The light is coming from a little box.

I am sure this box is not mine. It is small and round. It is white, and it looks old. It is about the size of an apple. There is a little star on the top. The star is silver. I do not touch the box at first. I only look at it.

Then there is a soft knock on my door.

“Nina?” my mother says. “Are you ready for bed?”

“Yes,” I say quickly.

“Brush your teeth again after your orange,” she says.

“I do not have an orange,” I answer.

“Oh,” she says. “Then good night soon.”

“Good night, Mum.”

I wait. Her steps go away. I look back at the box.

Then I touch it.

It is warm.

I pull it out from under the desk and put it on the floor in front of me. The little star shines softly. I sit and watch it for one minute, then two minutes, then three. Nothing happens. The box only shines.

I want to open it, but I also want to call my mother. I think about both things. Then I hear Tom laugh in the living room. I hear the television. I hear my father cough. Everything in the house is normal. Only my room is different.

So I put one hand on the box.

“Hello?” I say quietly.

The light becomes a little stronger.

I pull my hand back.

Then I hear a tiny voice.

“Hello,” the voice says.

I look all around my room. There is no one near the door. No one is at the window. No one is under the bed.

The voice comes again.

“Please do not be afraid.”

I look at the box. “Are you in the box?” I ask.

“Yes,” says the little voice.

I do not know what to say.

Then I say the first true thing in my head. “This is very strange.”

“Yes,” says the voice. “I know.”

The voice is small and kind. It sounds like a child, but also not like a child. I move closer to the box.

“Who are you?” I ask.

“My name is Pip,” the voice says.

“Are you very small?”

“Yes.”

“Are you a person?”

There is a little pause.

“I am a light keeper,” Pip says.

I do not understand. “What is a light keeper?”

“I keep small lights safe,” Pip says.

I look at the box, the star, and the soft white light. “Why are you in my room?”

“Because I need help.”

This is not the answer I want, but it is the answer I get. I sit on the floor and cross my legs.

“What kind of help?” I ask.

Then the box opens.

Very slowly, the top of the box moves up. Inside, there is more white light, and in the middle of it, I see Pip.

Pip is very small. Very, very small. He is smaller than my hand. He has a round face, bright eyes, and soft blue clothes. On his back there are two thin wings, like the wings of a small insect. They shine a little in the light. He is not scary. He looks tired.

I look at him. He looks at me.

“You are real,” I say.

“Yes,” he says.

“You can talk.”

“Yes.”

“You have wings.”

“Yes.”

I think for a moment. Then I say, “I think I am dreaming.”

Pip smiles a little. “No. You are awake.”

He stands inside the box and holds the side with one tiny hand.

“I lost a light,” he says.

I blink. “You lost a light?”

“Yes. A small room light.”

I look around my room. “Like my lamp?”

“No,” he says. “Not like that. This is a heart light.”

I do not understand again. That night I do not understand many things.

“What is a heart light?” I ask.

Pip points to my room. “Every room has one,” he says. “A heart light is small, but it is very important. It helps a room feel warm and safe. It helps people feel calm. It is not in every house in the same way. Some people do not see it. Some people feel it only. But it is there.”

I look around my room again. It is the same room as always. My bed is by the wall. My desk is by the window. My books are on the shelf. My yellow sweater is on the chair. But now the room feels different because Pip is in it and he is talking about hidden lights.

“And you lost mine?” I ask.

Pip looks sad. “Yes.”

“How?”

“I carry many little lights at night,” he says. “I take them where they need to go. Sometimes a new baby needs one. Sometimes an old room needs a new one. Sometimes a sad room needs a brighter one. Tonight I come here with yours, but I hear a cat outside. I look away. I move too fast. Then the light falls.”

“Where?”

Pip looks down.

“It is here in your room,” he says. “But I cannot find it.”

I look around with big eyes. “A heart light is lost in my room?”

“Yes.”

“Can we see it?”

“Maybe,” says Pip. “If we look well.”

Now I am not tired at all. I am wide awake. I stand up fast.

“Let’s look,” I say.

Pip flies out of the box. He is so small and light that the air moves only a little. He flies near my pillow first. Then near the shelf. Then near the curtain. I look under the bed, behind the chair, under the books, and near my shoes.

Nothing.

We look for a long time. I check every corner. I move my school bag. I open the drawer in my desk. I look in my slippers. Pip flies in small circles and watches carefully.

After some time, I sit on the bed and sigh.

“I can’t find it,” I say.

Pip sits on my pillow. He looks tired and worried. “I am sorry,” he says.

I look at him. “Why are you sorry?”

“Because the room may grow cold without its heart light.”

I touch my blanket. “It is not cold now.”

“Not now,” he says. “But little by little.”

I think about my room without its warm feeling. I think about no soft mornings, no calm nights, no happy reading on my bed. I do not want that.

So I stand up again.

“We try again,” I say.

Pip looks at me with hope. “Yes?”

“Yes,” I say. “We look again. Slowly.”

This time we do not look fast. We look slowly. We touch things gently. We move one thing at a time. I start with my desk. I take off my books. I take off my pens. I lift my lamp. I look behind my notebooks.

Nothing.

Then I go to the shelf. I touch every book. I move the small glass cup with my pencils. I move the photo of my family from the beach. I smile when I see the photo. Tom is very small in it. He has sand on his face. My mother is laughing. My father is holding a big hat.

Pip flies near the photo.

“Wait,” he says.

“What?”

He points to the frame. “There.”

Behind the photo frame, I see a soft gold light.

It is very small, like a tiny star.

I hold my breath.

“That is it?” I whisper.

“Yes,” Pip whispers too.

The little gold light does not stay still. It moves softly, like it is breathing. It is beautiful. I reach my hand to it, but Pip says, “Slowly.”

So I move very slowly.

The tiny gold light comes to my fingers.

It is warm.

It is warmer than tea, warmer than the morning sun on the window, warmer than socks from the fire. But it is not too hot. It feels kind.

Pip smiles for the first time. “You found it.”

I smile too. “Yes.”

“What do I do now?” I ask.

Pip flies back to the white box and opens his hands. “Please bring it here.”

I walk to the box with both hands around the little gold light. I do not want to drop it. I do not want it to go out. I put my hands over the box. Pip nods.

The little light leaves my hands and goes into the box.

At once, the whole box shines.

Not bright like the sun. Not strong like a car light. It shines in a soft, deep way, like the room is smiling. The walls look warmer. My books look nicer. My bed looks softer. Even the air looks sweet and quiet.

Pip laughs. It is a tiny laugh, but very happy.

“The room is well again,” he says.

I look all around. “It feels different.”

“Yes,” he says. “Now the heart light is in its place.”

“Where is its place?”

Pip points up.

I look at the ceiling, then the wall, then my lamp.

“I don’t see it,” I say.

“You do not always see it with your eyes,” says Pip. “Sometimes you feel it.”

I stand still. I feel the room. It is the same room, but also not the same. It feels safe. It feels soft. It feels like home.

Then I understand a little.

“Oh,” I say.

Pip smiles and nods. “Yes. Oh.”

We are both quiet for a moment.

Then I ask, “Do all rooms have one?”

“Many rooms do,” he says. “A kitchen can have one. A school room can have one. A baby room can have one. A room with love is a good place for a heart light.”

I think of our kitchen in the morning. I think of my mother’s voice. I think of my father’s coffee. I think of Tom talking and laughing. I think of my room at night with my books and blanket.

Then I ask, “Can a room lose its light again?”

Pip looks serious now. “Sometimes. When people are very sad for a long time. When a room is hard and angry. When no one cares for it.”

That makes me sad. I look at my desk. I look at the books on the shelf. I look at the chair with my yellow sweater. I do not want my room to feel lost or sad.

“I will care for my room,” I say.

Pip puts one tiny hand on his chest. “That helps very much.”

Then I hear steps in the hall.

“Nina?” my father says from outside the door. “Are you asleep?”

I jump.

Pip flies back into the box very fast. The light becomes smaller. The box closes.

“Yes!” I call.

“Good night,” my father says.

“Good night!”

I listen. His steps go away.

I turn back to the box and whisper, “That was close.”

Pip opens the box a little and peeks out. “Yes.”

I laugh softly.

Then I feel very tired all at once. The strange night, the talking, the looking, the little gold light, the secret box—now I feel all of it in my body.

Pip sees my face. “You should sleep.”

“Yes,” I say. “I think so too.”

He bows a little, like in an old story. “Thank you, Nina.”

I kneel by the box. “Will you go now?”

“Yes,” he says. “I have more lights to carry.”

I think of him in the night, flying from room to room with small warm lights. I like that picture very much.

“Will I see you again?” I ask.

“Maybe,” he says. “When a room needs help. Or when a heart light wants a friend.”

I smile. “Then I hope we meet again.”

Pip smiles too. “Sleep well.”

He closes the box. The white light grows soft, then softer, then very small. Then it goes out.

I wait.

The box is still there for one minute.

Then it is gone.

I do not know how. It is just not there anymore.

I sit on the floor and touch the place where it was. Only the floor is there. Nothing more.

I get into bed slowly and pull up my blanket. My room feels warm and safe. The shadows are soft. The air is calm. I close my eyes.

That night I sleep very well.

In the morning, I wake up at seven o’clock. The light from the window is soft and yellow. I hear birds outside. I hear my mother in the kitchen. I hear Tom in the hall. Everything is normal.

But I know something new.

I get out of bed and touch my desk. Then I touch the shelf. Then I touch the wall near my bed. I do not see a gold light. I do not hear a tiny voice. But I feel that my room is full of quiet warmth.

At breakfast, my mother says, “You look happy today.”

“I am happy,” I say.

Tom says, “Why?”

I think for a moment. I cannot tell him about Pip. It feels like a secret for now. So I say, “Because my room is nice.”

Tom laughs. “That is a funny reason.”

But my mother smiles. “A nice room is important,” she says.

“Yes,” I answer. “It is.”

All day at school, I think about the little white box and the tiny gold light. I think about rooms in the world. I think about warm kitchens, quiet bedrooms, and happy classrooms. I think maybe some places feel good because they have a heart light. And maybe some places need more care, more kindness, and more time.

That evening, after school, I clean my desk without my mother asking. I put my books in a nice line. I fold my sweater. I open the window for fresh air. I water the small plant on the shelf. Then I sit on my bed and smile.

My room smiles back in its quiet way.

Days go by. Then weeks go by. I do not see Pip again. But sometimes, at night, when the house is very still, I wake up for one second and think I see a tiny white light near the door or by the window. It is always gone very fast. Maybe it is Pip. Maybe it is another light keeper. Maybe it is only a dream.

But now I know this: small things can be very important.

A small light.

A small room.

A small act of care.

When my brother is sad, I sit with him. When my mother is tired, I help in the kitchen. When my father is quiet, I bring him tea. When my room is not clean, I clean it. When the window is closed all day, I open it. When my books are on the floor, I pick them up.

I think a heart light likes these things.

I think a home likes these things too.

Now, every night before bed, I stand in the middle of my room for one little minute. I look at my desk, my chair, my shelf, my bed, and the window. Then I say very softly, “Good night.”

I do not know if the room hears me.

I do not know if the heart light hears me.

But I think maybe it does.

And sometimes, when the night is very quiet, I think I hear a tiny voice say, very far away and very softly:

“Good night, Nina.”
