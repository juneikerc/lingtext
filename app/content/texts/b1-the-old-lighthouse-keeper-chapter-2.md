# The Old Lighthouse Keeper - Chapter 2: The Journey to St. Ives

The train journey from London to Cornwall took five hours, but Sarah barely noticed the time passing. She spent the entire trip staring out the window, watching the landscape change from gray city buildings to rolling green hills, and finally to the dramatic cliffs of the Cornish coast.

When she arrived at St. Ives station, the air smelled of salt and seaweed. The town was beautiful, with narrow cobblestone streets and white cottages that climbed up the hillsides. Fishing boats bobbed in the harbor, and seagulls cried overhead. It was nothing like the Yorkshire village her grandmother had described.

Sarah walked to the address on the letter, which led her to a small cottage on the edge of town. An old woman answered the door, leaning heavily on a wooden cane.

"You must be Sarah," the woman said, her voice soft and tired. "I'm Martha. I take care of Thomas. Come in, dear. He's been waiting for you."

The cottage was warm and smelled of lavender and old books. In a chair by the window sat a frail old man, his skin pale and his hands shaking. But when he saw Sarah, his eyes lit up.

"Margaret?" he whispered, his voice barely audible.

"No, I'm Sarah. Margaret's granddaughter."

Thomas's face fell, but then he smiled sadly. "Of course. You have her eyes. She always had such beautiful eyes."

Martha brought tea and then left them alone. Thomas gestured for Sarah to sit in the chair opposite him.

"I suppose you're wondering about the secret," he said.

"I want to understand who my grandmother really was. She never told me she had a brother. She never told me anything about Cornwall."

Thomas looked out the window at the gray sea. "Your grandmother was the bravest person I ever knew. And I destroyed her life."

"What do you mean?"

"It happened in 1984," Thomas began, his voice distant. "We were both young, just twenty-two years old. Our father was the lighthouse keeper at Godrevy Lighthouse. Do you know it?"

Sarah shook her head.

"It's an old lighthouse on the coast. Our family had kept it for three generations. It was isolated, lonely work, but our father loved it. And so did I."

Thomas paused to catch his breath. Sarah waited patiently.

"One night in November, there was a terrible storm. The winds were so strong that no boats could go out. But a ship had gotten caught on the rocks near the lighthouse. We could hear the sailors screaming for help."

Sarah leaned forward. "What happened?"

"Our father went out to try to save them. He took the small rescue boat. Margaret begged him not to go. She said the storm was too dangerous. But he wouldn't listen. He was a stubborn man."

Thomas's eyes filled with tears. "I was supposed to go with him, but I was afraid. I told him I was sick. So he went alone. The boat capsized. He drowned trying to save those sailors."

"That's not your fault," Sarah said gently. "You were afraid. That's natural."

"But that's not the whole story," Thomas said, his voice breaking. "Your grandmother blamed me. She said if I had gone with him, I could have helped. Maybe we both would have survived. Maybe he wouldn't have gone out alone. She was right."
