---
title: "Should Schools Start Later?"
level: "b2"
date: "2026-05-18"
---

In many countries, secondary school begins early in the morning, often before eight o'clock. For adults who are used to work schedules, this may seem normal. However, for teenagers, early starts can create a serious problem. Research suggests that adolescents naturally tend to fall asleep later and wake up later than younger children or adults. As a result, a school timetable that looks practical on paper may be working against students' health, attention, and learning.

The strongest argument for later school starts is sleep. Teenagers need enough rest to concentrate, remember information, and manage emotions. When classes begin very early, many students arrive tired before the day has even started. They may drink coffee or energy drinks to stay awake, but these habits do not solve the basic issue. A tired student can sit in a classroom and still learn very little. If schools want better academic results, they should not ignore the simple fact that learning requires an alert mind.

There is also a mental health dimension. Lack of sleep is connected to stress, low mood, and poor decision-making. Of course, changing the timetable will not solve every problem young people face. Students still need supportive teachers, reasonable homework, and safe homes. Nevertheless, sleep is one of the few factors that schools can influence directly. Starting classes even thirty or forty minutes later could make mornings less stressful for many families and reduce the number of students who begin the day already exhausted.

On the other hand, later starts create practical difficulties. School buses may need new schedules, parents may have to adjust work routines, and after-school activities could finish later in the evening. For students who care for younger siblings or work part-time, a later end to the school day might be inconvenient. These concerns are real, and they explain why some communities resist change. A timetable is not only an educational tool; it is connected to transport, family life, sports, and local employment.

However, practical difficulty should not be confused with impossibility. Schools regularly reorganize timetables for exams, building repairs, or special events. If student wellbeing is considered important enough, communities can test different models before making a permanent decision. For example, one school could begin with a trial period of two months. During that time, teachers could record attendance, students could report sleep habits, and parents could share concerns. Such evidence would be more useful than arguing from habit alone.

Another objection is that teenagers would simply stay up later if school started later. This might happen in some cases, especially if students spend long evenings online. Yet this argument places all responsibility on young people while ignoring biology and environment. Schools cannot control every bedtime, but they can avoid making healthy sleep unnecessarily difficult. Moreover, a later start could be combined with lessons about digital habits, homework planning, and the importance of rest.

Teachers might benefit as well. A class full of students who are half asleep is difficult to teach, and it can make even a well-planned lesson feel unsuccessful. If students arrive more rested, teachers may spend less time repeating instructions or dealing with low energy. Better mornings would not remove every classroom challenge, but they could make the first lessons of the day more productive and less frustrating for everyone involved.

In conclusion, schools should seriously consider starting later, especially for older students. The change would not be simple, and it would require careful planning. Nevertheless, the potential benefits for attention, health, and emotional balance are too important to dismiss. Education is not only about adding more hours of instruction. It is about creating conditions in which students are able to learn. A slightly later morning may be one of the most practical ways to do that.
