---
title: "The Last Evening at the Rooftop Garden"
level: "b2"
date: "2026-06-24"
---

For eight years, the rooftop garden above Tower Block C had been the most unlikely place in the neighborhood. The building itself was gray, practical, and forgettable, with narrow balconies and a lift that worked only when it felt generous. Yet on the roof, above the laundry lines and satellite dishes, there were tomato plants, mint bushes, wooden boxes of soil, and a small lemon tree that had survived three winters against all expectations.

Amira had discovered the garden during her first summer in the building. She had been seventeen then, newly arrived in the city, and embarrassed by how much she missed the village where she had grown up. The streets below were hot and impatient. The roof, by contrast, smelled of wet leaves and dust. It gave her a place to breathe.

The garden did not belong to anyone officially. Years earlier, an older resident had carried the first bags of soil upstairs after noticing that the roof was always empty. Others had joined gradually. Someone brought old paint buckets for herbs. Someone built a bench from broken furniture. Someone installed a rain barrel. Nobody asked permission, and for a long time nobody stopped them.

Then the notice appeared beside the lift. The building would be renovated in September. The roof would be closed for safety reasons. All personal items had to be removed by the end of the month.

At first, Amira felt only anger. The notice called the garden a collection of unauthorized materials, as if the plants were rubbish and the years of care meant nothing. But anger did not water tomatoes or move soil. So, during the final week of August, the residents climbed the stairs each evening and began to take the garden apart.

The work was heavier than anyone expected. Roots had grown through wooden boxes. Pots had become fragile in the sun. The bench resisted every attempt to move it, as if it had decided that the roof was its final home. People who had barely spoken in the hallway now worked side by side, carrying plants down twelve flights of stairs.

Amira took the lemon tree. It was too large for her apartment, but she could not leave it behind. The tree had produced only four lemons in eight years, yet everyone treated it as a miracle. She wrapped its pot in cloth, asked two neighbors to help her lift it, and placed it near her kitchen window.

On the last evening, the roof looked strangely naked. Without the plants, the city seemed louder and closer. The residents stood in a loose circle, tired and dusty, watching the sunset stain the windows of nearby buildings.

Amira expected the moment to feel like defeat. Instead, she noticed something different. The garden had not disappeared completely. Pieces of it had moved into apartments, balconies, kitchens, and courtyards. Mint would grow in one window. Tomatoes would climb another wall. The lemon tree would lean toward her kitchen light.

Months later, the renovated roof reopened with clean tiles, metal railings, and a sign explaining that access was limited. It was safer, brighter, and almost completely empty.

By then, however, small gardens had begun to appear throughout the building. A child grew beans in a plastic bottle near the stairwell. Someone placed basil beside the mailboxes. Amira's lemon tree survived the winter and produced one hard green fruit in spring.

The rooftop garden had been closed, but its habit had spread. What began as one hidden place above the city became many small acts of care inside it.
