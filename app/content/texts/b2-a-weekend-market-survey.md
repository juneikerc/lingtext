title: "A Weekend Market Survey"
level: "b2"
date: "2026-04-26"
---

Last Saturday, our class visited a local street market to do a small survey about shopping habits. We wanted to find out **how many** people buy fresh food every week and **how much** money they usually spend. At first, we thought the activity would be simple, but after a few interviews, we realized that people’s answers were more varied than we expected.

Our teacher asked us to prepare clear questions. For example, we asked, “**How many** times a week do you come to this market?” and “**How much** do you usually spend on fruit and vegetables?” Some people came only once a week, while others came three or four times. One woman told us that she did not know exactly **how much** she spent because prices changed every day. However, she knew **how many** bags of vegetables she normally bought: two bags for herself and one for her parents.

Then we spoke to a young couple who were buying ingredients for dinner. We asked them **how many** people they were cooking for. They said they were preparing a meal for six friends. After that, we asked **how much** rice they needed and **how much** chicken they planned to buy. They laughed because they had not made a proper list. They knew **how many** guests were coming, but they were not sure **how much** food would be enough.

Near the bakery, we interviewed an older man. He bought bread every morning, so we asked him **how many** loaves he bought per week. He answered that he bought seven loaves, one each day. Then we asked **how much** bread he ate himself and **how much** he shared with his family. He explained that he lived with his daughter and two grandchildren, so he had to think carefully about **how much** food was needed at home.

Later, we talked to a market seller. She told us **how many** customers usually visited her stand on a busy Saturday. She also explained **how much** time she spent preparing the products before opening. According to her, it took at least two hours to organize everything. She knew exactly **how many** boxes of tomatoes, onions, and peppers she had, but she could not always predict **how much** money she would earn by the end of the day.

By midday, we had collected a lot of information. We counted **how many** people preferred paying in cash and **how many** used a card. We also asked **how much** cash they normally carried. The results were interesting: younger customers often carried very little cash, while older customers usually carried more. This helped us understand not only **how many** people used each payment method, but also **how much** their habits depended on age.

At the end of the activity, we presented our findings in class. We explained **how many** interviews we had completed and **how much** useful information we had gathered. Our teacher said that the difference between **how many** and **how much** became much clearer because we had used the questions in real situations.

The project showed us that grammar is easier to remember when it is connected to daily life. When we talk about countable things, such as people, bags, loaves, or boxes, we use **how many**. When we talk about uncountable things, such as money, rice, bread, time, or food in general, we use **how much**.
